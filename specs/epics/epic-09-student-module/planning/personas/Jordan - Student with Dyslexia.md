## ðŸ˜“ Pain Points & Frustrations

### Reading & Text Challenges
1. **Text Processing Difficulties**
   - Reading is slow (2-3x longer than peers)
   - Letters/words swap or blur when stressed
   - Loses place frequently when reading long passages
   - Misreads dates, names, similar-looking words
   - Reading comprehension drops when fatigued

2. **Note-Taking Struggles**
   - Can't listen and write simultaneously (cognitive overload)
   - Handwriting is messy, hard to read later
   - Spelling errors in notes cause confusion
   - Skips words or scrambles word order
   - Organized note-taking systems are difficult to maintain

3. **Study Material Challenges**
   - Textbooks are overwhelming (500+ page history books)
   - Photocopied handouts have small fonts, poor contrast
   - Prefers audio but professors don't provide lecture audio
   - Flashcards are helpful but hard to make (spelling, typing time)
   - Study guides online are text-heavy

4. **Exam Anxiety**
   - Misreads essay prompts on exams
   - Runs out of time despite extended time accommodations
   - Worried about spelling errors in short answer sections
   - Multiple choice questions with similar wording are confusing

### Social & Emotional Challenges
- Feels "stupid" when reading takes longer (knows he's not, but hard)
- Avoids study groups (embarrassed about reading speed)
- Anxiety about being "found out" by classmates
- Exhaustion from compensating strategies (takes mental energy)
- Frustration with inaccessible digital tools (PDFs not screen-reader friendly)

## ðŸŽ“ Typical Use Cases

### Lecture Recording & Review Workflow
1. **Before Lecture**:
   - Sits near front (easier to focus)
   - Opens Notability, starts audio recording
   - Has syllabus open (knows topic, reduces cognitive load)

2. **During Lecture**:
   - Focuses on listening (doesn't attempt detailed notes)
   - Types occasional keywords or timestamps
   - Takes photos of slides with phone (reads later with TTS)

3. **After Lecture** (same day):
   - Re-listens to recording while following typed keywords
   - Uses TTS to read any handouts or slides
   - Tries to create structured notes but often gives up (overwhelming)

4. **Before Exam** (1 week later):
   - Re-listens to key lectures (time-consuming)
   - Struggles to create study materials from messy notes
   - Relies heavily on textbook audiobooks

### With Transcript Parser - Ideal Workflow
1. **During Lecture**:
   - Focuses entirely on listening and understanding
   - No note-taking pressure

2. **After Lecture** (on laptop at home):
   - Opens transcript with dyslexia-friendly font
   - Uses text-to-speech to read along (multi-modal input)
   - AI-generated summary gives overview
   - Important terms are highlighted (visual cues)

3. **Study Preparation**:
   - Generates flashcards automatically (spelling is correct)
   - Gets structured outline (organized better than his notes)
   - Exports to Quizlet, uses with TTS
   - Quiz questions for self-testing

4. **Before Exam**:
   - Reviews summaries (faster than full transcripts)
   - Listens to audio while reading transcript (reinforcement)
   - Confident he hasn't missed key information

## ðŸŽ¨ Design Implications

### UI/UX Preferences
- **Dyslexia-friendly fonts** - OpenDyslexic, Comic Sans, Arial (user choice)
- **High contrast modes** - Avoid pure white backgrounds
- **Larger text by default** - 14-16pt, easily adjustable
- **Clear visual hierarchy** - Headings, spacing, bullet points
- **Minimal clutter** - Clean, simple interfaces
- **Multi-modal input/output** - Audio + text + visual

### Feature Priorities (Ranked)
1. **Clean, formatted transcripts** - Proper spelling, punctuation, structure
2. **Integrated text-to-speech** - Read transcript aloud, sync highlighting
3. **AI-generated summaries** - Reduce reading load
4. **Visual highlighting** - Color-coded key concepts
5. **Dyslexia-friendly formatting** - Font, spacing, contrast options
6. **Audio + text sync** - Highlight words as audio plays
7. **Spell-tolerant search** - Find content despite misspellings
8. **Structured outlines** - Clear hierarchy, logical organization

### Accessibility Requirements (MUST HAVE)
- **WCAG AAA compliance** - Highest accessibility standard
- **Screen reader compatibility** - For TTS software
- **Keyboard navigation** - Full functionality without mouse
- **Customizable display** - Font, size, spacing, color
- **Audio descriptions** - For any visual-only content
- **Simple language** - Clear instructions, no jargon
- **Consistent layout** - Predictable structure reduces cognitive load

### Anti-Patterns (What to Avoid)
- Small fonts, low contrast (hard to read)
- Pure white backgrounds (causes visual stress)
- Justified text (uneven spacing confuses)
- Complex layouts (too much cognitive load)
- Text-only interfaces (no audio alternative)
- Spelling-exact search (excludes dyslexic users)
- Auto-correct without confirmation (changes intended words)

## ðŸ§  Dyslexia-Specific Considerations

### Strengths to Leverage
- **Strong auditory processing** - Remembers what he hears
- **Creative thinking** - Visual, big-picture thinker
- **Problem-solving** - Developed compensatory strategies
- **Determination** - Persistent despite challenges
- **Tech-savvy** - Learned to use assistive technology

### Common Dyslexia Patterns
- **Letter/word reversals** - b/d, saw/was, 1965/1956
- **Spelling difficulties** - Knows the word, can't spell it
- **Slow reading** - Decoding takes time, energy
- **Working memory** - Harder to hold info while processing
- **Sequencing** - Steps, timelines can be confusing

### Design Principles for Dyslexia
1. **Reduce reading load** - Summaries, audio alternatives
2. **Multi-sensory input** - See + hear + interact
3. **Clear structure** - Predictable, logical organization
4. **Spell-tolerant** - Fuzzy search, autocomplete
5. **Visual cues** - Icons, colors, spacing
6. **Chunking** - Break content into small pieces
7. **Repetition** - Reinforce key concepts multiple ways
